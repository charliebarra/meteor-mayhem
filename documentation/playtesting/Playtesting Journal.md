# Playtesting Journal

## Why I Started Playtesting

When I finished the first playable version, I thought the game was fairly balanced.

Then I realized I already knew how everything was supposed to work. New players did not.

Watching them made it possible to see problems I could no longer notice by myself.

## Observation 1 — Players Saved Everything

### What I expected

Players would spend fuel and resources whenever they needed them.

### What happened

Many players saved everything because they were worried they would need it later. That slowed the game down.

### What I changed

I added more reasons to spend resources earlier.

### What surprised me

The players were not making bad decisions. The rules were teaching them to hoard.

## Observation 2 — One Strategy Became Too Strong

### What I expected

Players would try several ways to win.

### What happened

Once someone found the strongest approach, other players copied it.

### What I changed

I adjusted costs and made other strategies more rewarding.

### What surprised me

Players solve games much faster than I expected.

## Observation 3 — The Rulebook Made Sense to Me

### What I expected

I thought the instructions were clear.

### What happened

Different players asked the same questions because I had skipped steps that felt obvious.

### What I changed

I reorganized parts of the rules and added examples where players kept getting stuck.

### What surprised me

Knowing the answer makes it hard to remember what a new player does not know yet.

## Observation 4 — Players Tried Things I Never Expected

### What I expected

People would mostly play the way I imagined.

### What happened

Some players took risks early. Others ignored upgrades I thought everyone would want. Someone built a strategy around a card I had barely considered.

### What I changed

I stopped trying to push players toward one “correct” approach.

### What surprised me

Players do not need the designer’s permission to be creative.

## Observation 5 — Small Changes Had Large Effects

Changing one cost, one resource, or one number could completely change what players chose to do.

Balancing was rarely one giant fix. It was usually a list of small changes, followed by another playtest.

Apparently, “balanced” is harder than it looks.

## Questions I Am Still Thinking About

- How much randomness feels exciting before it starts feeling unfair?
- How many strategies should be equally competitive?
- When does complexity make a game deeper, and when does it only make it harder to learn?
- How much information should players have before an important decision?
- What makes someone want to play one more game?
